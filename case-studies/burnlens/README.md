# BurnLens Case Study

**BurnLens** is a public-data wildfire planning concept used in OpenClaw Showcase to demonstrate how an agentic workflow can support geospatial decision-support thinking without overclaiming operational readiness.

This case study is part of a public-safe portfolio repository. It is not an emergency-response system, not a live wildfire monitoring tool, and not a production deployment.

BurnLens is included here to show how a research concept can move through a controlled workflow: scoping, drafting, traceability, QA review, limitation-setting, and human approval.

## What BurnLens Is

BurnLens explores how public geospatial data could be organized into a clearer planning narrative around wildfire risk, landscape context, and decision-support questions.

The concept is focused on questions like:

- What areas may deserve closer planning attention?
- What landscape or access factors might matter for wildfire planning?
- How can public geospatial layers be explained clearly to a non-specialist reader?
- How can an AI-assisted workflow help organize a bounded research artifact without overstating what the artifact proves?

In this showcase, BurnLens is treated as a **bounded public-data planning concept**, not as a predictive system or operational response platform.

---

## Why It Belongs in OpenClaw Showcase

BurnLens is useful as a case study because it connects three themes:

1. **Agentic workflow design**  
   The artifact is developed through scoped work, run receipts, QA review, and human approval.

2. **GeoAI direction**  
   The concept aligns with public-data geospatial AI interests, disaster planning, and trustworthy decision-support systems.

3. **Claim discipline**  
   Wildfire-related projects can easily overstate their usefulness. BurnLens is intentionally framed with limitations, review gates, and public-safety boundaries.

This makes BurnLens a good test case for OpenClaw’s workflow discipline: the value is not only in producing a polished artifact, but in showing how the artifact is scoped, reviewed, and limited.

## What This Case Study Demonstrates

BurnLens demonstrates how OpenClaw can support public-safe artifact development.

Specifically, it shows:

- How a technical concept can be scoped before drafting.
- How a geospatial idea can be presented without claiming operational authority.
- How limitations can be built into the artifact instead of added as an afterthought.
- How QA review can check both the output and the workflow.
- How human approval gates can prevent premature publication.
- How AI-assisted work can be made more traceable through run receipts.

## Public-Safe Framing

BurnLens should be described as:

```text
A public-data wildfire planning concept focused on geospatial decision-support thinking.
```

BurnLens should not be described as:

```text
A live wildfire response system.
A deployed emergency-management tool.
A predictive risk model.
A parcel-level risk assessment system.
A production-ready GeoAI platform.
```

This distinction is important. The showcase version of BurnLens is about workflow, research framing, and public-safe communication.

## What BurnLens May Include

A public-safe BurnLens artifact may include:

- A bounded planning question.
- A clearly defined area of interest.
- Public-data context.
- A high-level description of relevant geospatial factors.
- A map-card or visual explanation.
- An evidence ledger or source summary.
- Limitation statements.
- QA review notes.
- Portfolio-safe narrative text.

---

## What BurnLens Does Not Include

This showcase case study does not include:

- Live wildfire feeds.
- Emergency response recommendations.
- Real-time monitoring.
- Evacuation guidance.
- Parcel-level conclusions.
- Insurance, legal, or safety advice.
- Model performance claims.
- Deployment instructions.
- Private runtime details.
- Raw logs or private operational traces.

Any future technical expansion would need its own review, evidence, and safety boundaries before being described publicly.

## Example Planning Question

A public-safe BurnLens planning question might be:

> How can public geospatial data be organized into a clearer planning narrative for wildfire-prone landscapes, while keeping limitations visible and avoiding operational claims?

This question is intentionally bounded. It does not claim that BurnLens can predict fires, direct emergency response, or replace expert judgment.

---

## Review Questions

Before a BurnLens artifact is treated as portfolio-ready, it should be reviewed against questions like:

- Is the artifact clearly framed as a concept?
- Are public-data assumptions stated?
- Are limitations visible?
- Are unsupported claims removed?
- Does the artifact avoid operational language?
- Does it avoid emergency-response guidance?
- Does it avoid private runtime details?
- Does it connect back to the OpenClaw workflow?
- Has human approval been given before public release?

BurnLens is a public-data wildfire planning concept used to demonstrate OpenClaw’s agentic workflow discipline.
